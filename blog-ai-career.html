How to Actually Break Into AI/ML in India in 2025
(Not the Version LinkedIn Tells You)

Everyone gives the same advice. Do certifications. Learn Python. Solve LeetCode. Get a CGPA above 8. Here's what actually worked — from a B.Tech grad with a 6.69 CGPA from Warangal who now works on Apple's LLM.

Let me tell you exactly where I started. B.Tech CSE from a tier-3 college in Warangal. CGPA of 6.69. No IIT, no NIT, no MAANG internship. No famous professor who guided me, no family connections in tech, no city advantage.

By the time I was two years out of college, I was a Prompt Engineer working on Apple's LLM — one of the most tightly scrutinised AI systems on the planet — and building my own startups on the side.

I'm not telling you this to brag. I'm telling you this because the standard career advice is optimised for people who already have an advantage. This is written for people who don't.

First: The Myths That Are Actively Hurting You

Myth

"You need a high CGPA to get into AI/ML."

Reality

CGPA matters for campus placements at tier-1 companies. For everything else — freelance work, startup roles, annotation and prompt engineering contracts, and most ML jobs outside the big four — nobody has ever asked me for my CGPA. They asked what I'd built.

Myth

"You need a deep learning specialisation / Andrew Ng's course to get started."

Reality

Courses give you vocabulary. Projects give you understanding. I've met people who've done 12 Coursera certificates and can't explain why you'd use SVM over XGBoost. Build something. Then, if you need theory to explain what you built, go get the theory.

Myth

"Prompt engineering isn't a real technical skill."

Reality

Prompt engineering at the level of writing SFT training data for production LLMs requires deep understanding of how language models learn, what makes a response better or worse, safety taxonomy, multi-turn conversation design, and domain-specific formatting. It is harder than most software engineering roles and pays extremely well globally.

The Single Best Advice — Deploy Something Live

Here it is. The one thing that changed everything for me, that I see almost no fresher doing, that immediately separates you from 90% of applicants:

"Build a project. Train a model. Wrap it in a Flask API. Deploy it somewhere with a real URL. Then put that URL in your resume."

Not a Jupyter notebook. Not a Colab link. Not a GitHub repo with a README that says "run main.py." A live URL that anyone can visit, interact with, and see working in real time.

This matters because it proves three things simultaneously:

  • You understand the full stack — not just training, but serving a model as an API
  • You care about the user experience — someone can use what you built without instructions
  • You can finish things — most ML students start projects; very few deploy them

IBM Cloud has a free tier. Vercel is free for web apps. Railway is free for Flask APIs. There is no cost barrier. The only barrier is the 4–6 hours it takes to figure out deployment the first time.

The 2025 Entry Points Nobody Is Talking About

The AI industry has created several entry points that didn't exist three years ago. Most freshers don't know about them or underestimate them.

1. AI Annotation & Data Labelling

Platforms like Oneforma, Scale AI, Appen, and Telus Digital hire for annotation work that is increasingly sophisticated — audio annotation, OCR, handwriting, video labelling, and prompt evaluation. This is how I started with Centific. The work builds your understanding of what AI systems need to function, which is different from and complementary to what courses teach you.

2. Prompt Engineering Contracts

Since late 2023, there has been consistent demand for people who can write high-quality SFT data for LLMs. This is remote, pays well by Indian standards, and is genuinely interesting technical work. The entry point is demonstrating that you can write a good prompt — not just a clever one, but a diverse, safe, well-formatted, instruction-following example that an LLM can actually learn from.

3. Build-In-Public → Get Hired

Three engineers I know personally got their first ML roles because someone from a startup saw their project on LinkedIn and reached out. Build your projects. Write one paragraph about what you learned. Post it. Do this consistently for 90 days. The compound effect is real.

Your 90-Day Roadmap (From Zero to Hireable)

1
Days 1–20 — Python + pandas + scikit-learn fundamentals
Not from a course — from building. Pick a dataset from Kaggle (heart disease, fraud, weather). Clean it. Visualise it. Train a simple model. Understand every line.
2
Days 21–45 — Build your first deployed ML app
Take your model, wrap it in Flask, deploy on Railway or IBM Cloud. It doesn't have to be pretty. It has to have a URL. Put the URL on your resume immediately.
3
Days 46–60 — Learn what makes a prompt good
Read OpenAI's, Anthropic's, and Google's prompt engineering documentation. Then write 20 SFT examples for different task categories — coding, summarisation, Q&A, safety. Focus on diversity and quality, not quantity.
4
Days 61–75 — Apply to annotation and prompt engineering work
Oneforma, Scale AI, Appen, Outlier AI, Telus Digital. These are real contracts that pay real money and build real experience. Your 20 SFT examples are your application portfolio.
5
Days 76–90 — Build in public
Post one project update per week on LinkedIn. Write what you built, what broke, what you learned. Tag it with #MachineLearning #AI #Python. You will be surprised who notices.

What About CGPA, Tier of College, City?

These things matter for some doors and not at all for others. The prompt engineering and annotation entry points are almost entirely portfolio and skill based. The AI startup ecosystem in Hyderabad, Bangalore, and Pune is growing fast and genuinely values demonstrated skills over pedigree.

The harder truth: location and tier matter less every year. Remote AI work has been normalised. A deployed project with a live URL has the same domain name whether you built it in Warangal or Whitefield.

One final thing: The AI field moves fast enough that the people who started 18 months ago are already senior to people who started 6 months ago. In a field this young, starting is the biggest advantage. The best time to start was a year ago. The second best time is right now.
