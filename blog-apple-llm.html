What Nobody Tells You About Writing Prompts for Apple's LLM

I've spent over a year writing supervised fine-tuning data for one of the most scrutinised language models on the planet. Here's what actually surprised me — and what the job descriptions never mention.

Ramana Vemunoori

When people hear "Prompt Engineer on the Apple LLM Project," they usually picture someone typing clever instructions into ChatGPT all day. The reality is both more technical and more human than that — and it surprised me in ways I didn't expect, even after two years in AI.

I'm going to be honest about everything that caught me off guard. Not to impress you. Just because I spent too long looking for a resource like this before I started, and it didn't exist.

Surprise #1 — Safety Is Not Binary. It's Deeply Contextual.

Before this role, I thought safety in AI was simple: some things are allowed, some things aren't. You learn the list, you apply it. That mental model lasted about two days.

The reality is that the same information can be completely fine or completely harmful depending on framing, context, and who's asking. A prompt about medication dosages written by a concerned parent reads entirely differently from the same question phrased as a method. A chemistry question from a student looks different from one embedded in a threatening narrative.

The key insight: Safety taxonomy is not a list of forbidden words. It's a framework for understanding intent, context, and downstream impact simultaneously. You have to hold all three in your head for every single prompt you write or evaluate.

What this means in practice: I would sometimes write a perfectly valid educational prompt about a sensitive topic, then have to classify it, then realise the classification changed the moment I changed one word in the phrasing. Then change it back. Then change the response instead. Then reconsider the category.

It's genuinely hard cognitive work. And it never gets fully automatic, because each edge case is slightly different from the last one.

Surprise #2 — Multi-Turn Conversations Are an Entirely Different Craft

Single-turn prompts — one question, one answer — feel manageable once you get the format right. Multi-turn conversations broke my brain in an entirely new way.

The challenge isn't length. It's context maintenance across topic transitions. A good multi-turn dataset needs to show the model how to:

  • Remember what was said three turns ago without explicitly referencing it
  • Gracefully change direction when the user shifts topic mid-conversation
  • Handle clarifications that partially contradict earlier instructions
  • Recover from its own earlier mistakes when the user corrects it
  • Know when to ask for clarification vs. when to make a reasonable assumption

Each of those behaviours needs to be demonstrated through the data. You can't just tell the model to do these things — you have to show it, in the form of example conversations where a good assistant does exactly that, turn by turn.

"You can't tell the model how to handle ambiguous corrections. You have to write a conversation where it happens perfectly, so the model learns by example."

Designing those edge cases — imagining the exact user message that creates the problem, then writing the ideal assistant response — is the hardest part of this job. It took me several months to get genuinely good at it.

Surprise #3 — Coding Prompts and Math Prompts Are Completely Different Disciplines

I assumed writing technical prompts would feel consistent. Code is code, math is math — right?

Wrong. Coding prompts and math prompts require almost opposite thinking.

💻
Coding Prompts
Need real, runnable code in the response. Need to cover edge cases in the problem statement. Need to specify language, version, and constraints. The format is everything — indentation, comments, structure.
🔢
Math Prompts
KaTeX notation for every formula. Step-by-step reasoning shown explicitly. The process matters more than the answer. A correct answer with bad reasoning is a failed example.

And then there's the intersection — algorithmic problems that need both code and mathematical explanation. Those require you to context-switch mid-response between two completely different modes of precision.

The Post-ITN format added another layer: normalising spoken numbers, units, dates, and currencies the way a text-to-speech system expects them — which is often the opposite of how you'd naturally write them.

# Post-ITN example — written for TTS normalisation # Natural: "The temperature was 38.5°C on March 14th, 2024" # Post-ITN: "The temperature was thirty-eight point five degrees Celsius on the fourteenth of March twenty twenty-four" # KaTeX example in SFT response # The quadratic formula is: $$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$ # Each step must be shown, not just the final answer

Surprise #4 — The Sheer Diversity of Categories

Before I started, I thought prompt engineering was mainly about writing good questions and answers. I didn't realise how many types of tasks the model needs to handle, and how different each one feels to write.

In a single week I might write prompts covering:

  • Summarisation of technical documents (concise, no hallucination)
  • Creative rewriting with specific tone constraints
  • Information extraction from structured and unstructured text
  • Tool use and function calling scenarios
  • Multi-step reasoning chains for logic problems
  • Code generation and debugging in 3 different languages
  • Safety edge cases requiring careful dual-use analysis
  • Instruction following with conflicting or ambiguous constraints

Each category has its own conventions, its own failure modes, and its own quality signals. You learn them all simultaneously, and after a few months you start to feel when a response is subtly wrong in a way you can't immediately articulate — and then have to articulate it anyway, precisely, in the evaluation rationale.

Surprise #5 — Building the Team Was Part of the Job

At some point I noticed gaps in our team's coverage — certain categories were consistently weaker because not enough people had the right combination of skills to write them well. I hadn't been asked to fix this. I just started doing it.

I designed an evaluation framework to test candidates on prompt diversity, instruction-following quality, LLM knowledge, guideline adherence, and category coverage. Then I ran interviews. Then I onboarded the people who passed.

Nobody told me this was my job. But the work needed doing, and I could see it needed doing, so I did it. That might be the most unexpected thing about the role — how much initiative the best work requires.

If you want to do this work: Start writing SFT pairs now, before you get hired. Pick a category — coding, or math, or safety — and write 20 high-quality examples. The difference between a candidate who says "I understand SFT" and one who shows you 20 real examples is enormous.

What I'd Tell Someone Starting Out

The skills that matter most in this job are not the ones that appear in the job posting. Technical writing precision matters more than knowing the latest model architectures. The ability to hold ambiguity without resolving it prematurely matters more than knowing all the safety categories by name. Intellectual honesty about your own uncertainty matters more than confidence.

And the work compounds. The first month felt like learning a new language. By month six, I was writing prompts that I genuinely believe made the model better for real users — and I could feel the difference in the quality of what I was producing.

That feeling is worth a lot.
